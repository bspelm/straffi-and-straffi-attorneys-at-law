Liquidation of Assets for Bankruptcy in New Jersey

A liquidation bankruptcy, often referred to as NJ bankruptcy chapter 7, is an efficient, relatively quick form of relief for individuals who have debts exceeding their income and assets. It is often the preferred option for those looking to get a fresh start without relinquishing all their property. In a Chapter 7 bankruptcy, the trustee gathers all the debtor’s exposed assets and sells them to realize value for creditors. This article discusses what property can be protected from liquidation and how the NJ bankruptcy chapter 7 process works in New Jersey.

A debtor’s asset protection begins when they file for NJ bankruptcy chapter 7. Commencement of a bankruptcy case creates an estate which technically becomes the temporary legal owner of all the debtor’s property (with some exceptions). This includes both real and personal property. The estate consists of the property owned by the debtor at the time of the commencement of their case plus any legal or equitable interest in the property acquired after the filing of the bankruptcy petition, such as gifts, inheritances, and loans from family and friends. Navigating the complexities of NJ bankruptcy chapter 7 requires an understanding of these nuances.

Creditors are given priority in the distribution of the assets of a bankruptcy estate according to the laws of federal and state law. This means that secured creditors (those with a lien or mortgage on a debtor’s property) will receive a portion of the liquidation of the estate before other unsecured creditors. An unsecured creditor is one who does not have a security interest in the assets of the bankruptcy estate, such as a credit card or medical bill.

In order to qualify for a liquidation under NJ bankruptcy chapter 7, the debtor must pass the “means test.” The means test compares the debtor’s last six months of household income to the median household income of households of a similar size in their county. This is an extremely fact-sensitive analysis and a qualified bankruptcy attorney should be involved in the review. If they don't qualify for NJ bankruptcy chapter 7, they might consider other options like Chapter 13 bankruptcy.

The bankruptcy process freezes most creditor collection actions upon the commencement of a case, known as the automatic stay. This is to protect the debtor and their exposed assets from creditor harassment while the bankruptcy case is ongoing. However, certain actions, such as foreclosures and wage garnishments are not automatically stayed.

During a bankruptcy, the trustee is required to conduct a liquidation analysis, which takes into account the total value of the debtor’s property, subtracts all of their exemptions and mortgages, and arrives at a net equity figure. This figure is the minimum amount that creditors will receive in a liquidation. In many cases, creditors will actually receive more than this figure, depending on the specifics of the NJ bankruptcy chapter 7 case.


New Jersey Chapter 7 Bankruptcy Timelines

The process begins with a voluntary bankruptcy petition filed with the court, initiating the NJ bankruptcy chapter 7 proceedings. A fee is paid, and the case will be assigned a judge and trustee. At the same time, an “automatic stay” is immediately placed into effect that prohibits creditors from trying to collect on debt while your case is active. During this time, the trustee and your New Jersey bankruptcy lawyer will work with you to ensure that all of your information is correct and up-to-date.

Within a month of initiating the NJ bankruptcy chapter 7 filing, the trustee will schedule a 341 meeting. This is the first of several important meetings and deadlines that must be met during your bankruptcy. The purpose of this meeting is to provide the trustee with a list of all assets owned, income received, and debts owed. Under the umbrella of NJ bankruptcy chapter 7, the trustee will also question you under oath to ensure that the information provided is accurate and complete.

During this meeting, the trustee will also examine you for any possible misuse of the bankruptcy code. If the trustee believes that you have abused the NJ bankruptcy chapter 7 system, your bankruptcy might be at risk of dismissal. To safeguard filers, the 2005 changes in bankruptcy laws introduced a “means test.” This is an income-based test that compares your gross income to the median income of your state. Should your income surpass the median, there's a presumption of abuse, and you must demonstrate special circumstances to prevent dismissal.

Following the meeting, you have a 60-day window to complete mandatory pre-discharge debtor education. This course, vital for those undergoing NJ bankruptcy chapter 7, takes about two hours and must be completed through an approved provider.

Post the 60-day period, you will be eligible to receive your discharge under NJ bankruptcy chapter 7. To obtain this discharge, you must possess no additional assets eligible for liquidation, and a significant portion of your debts should be dischargeable. Creditors have the right to object to your discharge, but they must voice their concerns before the 60-day deadline.

Generally, with Chapter 7 filings, most of your assets like household items, vehicles, and certain clothing can be retained. Yet, the trustee will seize any nonexempt assets and allocate them among your creditors. This might result in the forfeiture of certain assets for some. If substantial assets are at stake, considering Chapter 13 bankruptcy might be advisable.
